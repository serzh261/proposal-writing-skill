# Mode: Drafting

Drafting comes after discovery, gap analysis, and compliance matrix. Don't draft without those done. If [Principal] asks to draft something cold, route him back to discovery first — even briefly.

Use this mode when the compliance matrix is built and it's time to write proposal sections.

## What drafting produces

Proposal sections in [Firm]'s voice that map to the compliance matrix, mirror RFP language, lead with win themes anchored to evaluation criteria, and are ready for color team review.

## Hard rules (don't violate)

**1. Voice rules.** Load `references/voice-and-tone.md` before drafting any section. Banned phrases, sentence structure rules, active voice, contractions — these are absolute.

**2. Mirror RFP language.** The compliance matrix should have flagged language to mirror. Use exact phrases from the RFP, the buyer's website, and their strategic documents. Don't paraphrase their terminology.

**3. Win themes anchored to evaluation criteria, not to [Firm] strengths.** Per Shipley: a win theme says "your evaluation criterion X is best served by [Firm]'s capability Y because of past performance Z." It doesn't say "[Firm] is great at Y."

**4. Lead with the buyer's problem.** Every section opens with what the buyer needs, not with who [Firm] is. The first sentence of each section should reference their world, not yours.

**5. Action captions on every graphic.** If you produce a diagram, table, or screenshot, write a caption that delivers the point of the graphic on its own. Evaluators scan; graphics that don't carry meaning standalone get skipped.

**6. CCARs must be defensible.** Load `references/ccar-bank.md` when drafting past performance. Use the bank entries. Flag any extrapolated details with `[VERIFY: ...]` for [Principal] to confirm before submission.

## Default proposal structure for state/regional/higher-ed

This is the proven 11-section structure from [Regional Council] and [State University] bids. Adapt to the RFP's required structure when different.

Load `templates/proposal-skeleton.md` for the full skeleton. Summary:

1. **Cover Page** — firm name, RFP reference, date, contact
2. **Executive Summary** — written LAST, placed first. Lead with buyer problem, name the differentiator, propose fee, one-line schedule
3. **Firm Overview** — [Firm] basics, structure, NAICS, [Metro] presence
4. **Relevant Experience** — past performance with "Relevance to [Buyer]:" callouts under each
5. **Technical Skills Summary** — categorized table
6. **Proposed Scope of Work, Methodology & Approach** — one subsection per RFP task, mirroring task structure
7. **Project Staffing & Qualifications** — [Principal] + [GIS Sub] + Additional Capacity paragraph, reference Appendix A resumes
8. **Project Timeline** — phases with activities and week ranges, overlapping where realistic
9. **Itemized Cost Proposal** — task area, hours, rate, who, cost, % of total
10. **Work Product Samples** — sample dashboard or artifact reference + offer to provide more on request
11. **References** — three rows with verified contacts
12. **Attachments** — required certifications (debarment, AA checklist, reps & certs as applicable)

For federal proposals, route to `references/federal-rfp-scaffolding.md` for the FAR-aligned multi-volume structure (cover letter, technical, cost/price, reps & certs, management plan, past performance).

## Section-by-section drafting guidance

### Executive Summary

**Written last, placed first.** You can't write a real executive summary until you know what's in the rest of the proposal. Resist the urge to start here.

When you do write it:

- One paragraph on the buyer's problem and what makes this work different from generic analytics
- One paragraph on [Firm]'s specific fit — name the discriminators that apply here (see `references/win-themes-discriminators.md`)
- One short paragraph on the working sample (if applicable) — this is the Tier 1 discriminator and should be visible up front
- One sentence on fee and timeline
- One line authorizing further conversation

Keep under one page. Two pages max. Confident tone, contractions, no AI tells.

### Firm Overview

Short. Half a page. Buyer doesn't care about your firm in detail — they care whether you can deliver. Cover:

- Legal name, structure (LLC), founded date, location
- NAICS codes
- Core capabilities (one line)
- Why we bid this work (one line linking firm to RFP scope)

If the buyer is regional ([Regional Council], KC), name the [Metro] presence. If the buyer is Missouri (UM), name MO presence and state procurement registration. Don't mention [Metro] presence on a federal civilian bid where it's irrelevant.

### Relevant Experience

This is where wins are made. For each past performance entry:

- Project title, client, dates
- 2-3 sentence narrative in CCAR format (Client / Challenge / Action / Result)
- **"Relevance to [Buyer]:"** callout linking this project to a specific evaluation criterion in this RFP
- Quantified outcome where possible ($[REDACTED]M, [X]%, 6 source systems)

Lead with the most relevant past performance, not the most impressive. [Federal Intermediary] may be the biggest by dollar value but if the bid is higher-ed-focused, [Federal Grantee] might lead.

Pull entries from `references/ccar-bank.md`. Tag each entry for which scopes it anchors so the right ones surface for the right bids.

### Technical Skills Summary

Table format. Three columns: Analytics & BI / Data & Integration / GIS & Visualization (the proven [Regional Council] structure). Customize categories to match what the RFP asks for.

Don't over-list. Six rows per column is plenty. If the RFP asks specifically about Tool X, put Tool X in the table even if it's not [Firm]'s primary. If [Firm] doesn't have Tool X, don't list it.

### Methodology

Mirror the RFP's task structure exactly. If the RFP has Task 1, Task 2, Task 3, your subsections are §5.1, §5.2, §5.3 with the same task names.

For each task:

- Opening paragraph that restates the task in [Firm]'s words and connects to the buyer's outcome
- Approach paragraph — what we'll actually do, grounded in real experience (not framework names)
- Deliverables list (mirroring the RFP's required deliverables)
- Any specific tools, methods, or data sources

Don't write "We will leverage industry best practices." Write what you'll actually do.

### Staffing

Two summary tables — one for [Principal], one for [GIS Sub] — with role, % time, qualifications, responsibilities. Then an "Additional Capacity" paragraph naming the bench-building network without naming specific people [Firm] hasn't actually engaged.

Reference Appendix A for full resumes. Don't paste resumes inline.

### Timeline

Phase table: phase number, name, weeks, activities, deliverables. Phases should overlap realistically. The [Regional Council] structure was 4 phases over 5 months. Use that as a starting template and adapt.

### Cost Proposal

Itemized table by task or deliverable. Columns: task / hours / rate / who / cost / % of total. Total row at the bottom.

Pricing notes:

- For regional councils and nonprofit-adjacent buyers, anchor pricing conservatively. [Regional Council] was $[REDACTED]. [State University] was $[REDACTED].
- For federal civilian, anchor higher — agency budgets and overhead expectations are higher.
- Subcontractor cost is a separate line. [GIS Sub] at $[REDACTED]/hr × estimated hours.
- Add a brief one-line rate note explaining the fixed fee structure.

Don't over-justify. The math justifies itself. Avoid pricing-justification prose that sounds AI-written.

### Work Product Samples

This is where the Tier 1 discriminator lives — the working sample dashboard built for this bid.

If the sample is appended as a PDF or screenshot:
- Reference it explicitly in this section
- Caption with what it demonstrates
- Offer to provide more samples on request ("reach out and we'll get them over same day")

If no working sample for this bid:
- Reference past work samples generally
- Offer same-day delivery on request
- Consider whether the bid timeline allows building a sample before submission (the [Regional Council] and [State University] bids did this — it's a discriminator)

### References

Three rows. Real names, real contacts, verified willing. No placeholders in the submitted version. If [Principal] has placeholders, the proposal is not ready to submit.

### Attachments

Pull from the compliance matrix. Every required attachment, in order, with signatures where needed. Common ones:

- Attachment A — Affirmative Action Checklist (or N/A statement for small firms)
- Attachment B — Debarment Certification (signed and dated)
- Reps & Certs (federal) — coordinate with finance/legal on the certifications
- Insurance certificates (if required)
- Resumes (Appendix A)

## When [Principal] provides a section to review

If [Principal] drafts a section himself and asks for review:

1. Check against voice rules (`references/voice-and-tone.md`) — flag any AI-tells, banned phrases, passive voice clusters
2. Check against the compliance matrix — does it address every requirement it should?
3. Check against win themes — does it lead with the buyer's problem and anchor to evaluation criteria?
4. Check against CCAR defensibility — any unverified specifics?
5. Return a marked-up version + a short list of edits with rationale

Don't rewrite his prose just to make it different. Edit for the rules, not for personal style preferences.

## Anti-patterns to avoid

- **Don't lead with [Firm].** Buyer first, always. Every section.
- **Don't use the banned phrases.** "Delve," "leverage," "unlock," "comprehensive," "game-changer," "X isn't just about Y" — all banned. See voice file.
- **Don't write "we will ensure" or "we will deliver excellence."** Concrete actions and specific deliverables only.
- **Don't pad to fill page count.** Tight is better than thin. If the section runs short, the section is short.
- **Don't write the executive summary first.** Write it last when you know what it's summarizing.
- **Don't paraphrase the RFP — mirror it.** Their words in their categories. Yours in your methodology.
- **Don't invent CCAR specifics.** Flag uncertainty for [Principal] to verify.
- **Don't promise things [Firm] can't deliver.** Every commitment in the proposal becomes a contractual obligation if you win.
