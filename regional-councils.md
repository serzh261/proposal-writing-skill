## What they value

Based on [Regional Council] RFP, broader regional council patterns, and the relationship [Principal] has built:

1. **Local presence and accessibility.** They serve KC. They expect [Metro] bidders or at least bidders with [Metro] operational capability. In-person meetings and same-day responsiveness matter.

2. **Microsoft-native tools.** [Regional Council]'s existing tech is Microsoft-based (Power BI, SharePoint, Azure). They value contractors who fit their stack rather than introducing new platforms.

3. **Conservative pricing.** Regional council nonprofit budgets are tight. [Regional Council] was won at $[REDACTED] — significantly below the $90-100K that other tools suggested. Don't price like federal civilian.

4. **Practical, not theoretical.** They want a contractor who'll embed with their staff, attend planning sessions, and turn raw operational data into usable documentation. Not a theoretical framework consultant.

5. **Multi-jurisdictional coordination experience.** [Regional Council]'s value is coordinating across 9 counties and dozens of municipalities. Past performance demonstrating multi-jurisdictional analytical work resonates strongly.

6. **Clean handoff post-engagement.** They don't want vendor lock-in or ongoing dependency. Self-sufficiency after handoff is a virtue.

## What they don't care about (or value less)

- DC consultancy pedigree
- Federal prime contractor pedigree (most [Regional Council] work is federally funded but procured locally)
- Brand-name partnerships (Microsoft Premier, Tableau Partner — not signals here)
- Large team headcount (they expect lean engagement)
- Multi-year frameworks and methodologies with formal names (Shipley, PMI, etc.) — they value execution

## Win themes that work for [Regional Council]-type bids

Anchor to typical regional council evaluation criteria:

**Theme 1 ([Metro] presence + local credibility):**
> "[Buyer]'s effectiveness depends on close coordination with [their member jurisdictions]. [Firm] operates from [City], attends [region] in-person, and brings [Metro Area] context that out-of-region firms can't match."

**Theme 2 (Microsoft-native fit):**
> "Buyer's existing technology investment is Microsoft-based. [Firm] is Microsoft-native end-to-end — Power BI, Azure Data Factory, SharePoint, Fabric — meaning faster onboarding, no platform transition, and reusable expertise after handoff."

**Theme 3 (multi-jurisdictional past performance):**
> "[Firm]'s lead consultant ran multi-jurisdictional federal program analytics at [Federal Intermediary Org] (a $[REDACTED]M initiative). The pattern of coordinating across multiple jurisdictions, harmonizing data, and producing executive-grade reporting maps directly to [Buyer]'s scope."

**Theme 4 (working sample as discriminator):**
> "Where most proposals describe analytics capability in words, [Firm] shows it. The [Buyer]-themed dashboard included as Appendix [X] is built specifically for this engagement and demonstrates the visual quality, design choices, and data fluency [Buyer] should expect from us."

## Proposal structure for [Regional Council]-type bids

Standard 11-section structure works (see `templates/proposal-skeleton.md`). Specific [Regional Council]-pattern notes:

**Cover page:** Include "Submitted to: [Buyer], [Address]" prominently. Date the bid was submitted.

**Executive Summary:** Lead with local-presence + Microsoft-fit. Name the working sample. State the fixed fee. ≤1 page.

**Firm Overview:** Half page. [Metro] presence highlighted. NAICS codes. LLC structure. Founded date. Don't oversell — buyers know small firms have lean structures and that's fine.

**Relevant Experience:** Lead with [Federal Intermediary] (multi-jurisdictional federal — directly relevant). Include enterprise sales pipeline (BI delivery at scale). Include any prior regional/government work. "Relevance to [Buyer]:" callouts under each.

**Technical Skills:** Three-column table (Analytics & BI / Data & Integration / GIS & Visualization). Microsoft tools first.

**Methodology:** Mirror the RFP's task structure exactly. For each task, name the deliverable, the approach, and the experience that supports it. "We'll embed with [Buyer] staff from day one" is the canonical opening — [Firm] voice, specific commitment.

**Staffing:** [Principal] + [GIS Subcontractor] tables. Add Additional Capacity paragraph naming the sourcing network. Reference Appendix A resumes.

**Timeline:** Phase table. 4 phases over the period of performance is typical. Phases should overlap realistically. End with a deliverable date that matches the RFP's required completion.

**Cost:** Fixed fee, itemized. Conservative anchor — for [Regional Council]-sized work, $70-95K range. Subcontractor (Ben) costs as separate line.

**Work Samples:** Reference the sample dashboard. Caption. Offer same-day delivery of additional samples.

**References:** Three real names. [Metro Area] contacts preferred (resonates with local presence theme). Verified within 30 days of submission.

**Attachments:** Affirmative Action Checklist (N/A statement for small firms). Debarment Certification (signed). Resumes (Appendix A). Work sample (Appendix B if separate file, or embedded in proposal).

## Anti-patterns specific to [Regional Council]-type bids

- **Don't price like a federal civilian bid.** Regional council budgets are tight; over-pricing knocks you out of consideration before anyone reads the technical approach.
- **Don't promise on-site without confirming it's needed.** [Regional Council] bids accept hybrid/remote. Ask in Q&A.
- **Don't use federal contracting language (FAR, CPARS) on local council bids.** They don't care.
- **Don't oversell the firm.** Two-person firms reading like 200-person firms trigger skepticism.
- **Don't skip the Q&A submission window.** Submitting clarifying questions during the Q&A period signals engagement and surfaces useful intel.
