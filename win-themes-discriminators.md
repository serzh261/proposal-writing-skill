# Win Themes and Discriminators

## The Shipley distinction (drilled in from research)

**Feature:** specific aspect or attribute of what you offer — the "what." Speed, schedule, tools used.

**Benefit:** tangible client advantage — the "so what." Reduced cost, faster delivery, lower risk.

**Strength:** a feature with a benefit that solves a high-importance client problem with low risk.

**Discriminator:** a benefit important to the client and *unique to you*. A real discriminator passes three tests:
1. The client cares about it
2. You can credibly claim it
3. Competitors cannot credibly claim it

Most "discriminators" small firms believe they have are actually features. Be honest about which is which.

## [Firm]'s Tier 1 Discriminators (use on every bid)

### Tier 1.A — Working sample built per bid

**Why it's a real discriminator:** Most competitors submit text-only proposals with at most a generic mockup. [Firm] builds an actual working dashboard or artifact specifically for the bid in the buyer's branding and using their public data. [Regional Council] and [State University] bids both included real samples. The pattern works.

**Why it passes the three tests:**
- Buyers care because they're tired of vendors who over-promise and under-deliver. A sample de-risks the decision.
- [Firm] can credibly claim it (proven twice).
- Most competitors won't bother — the time investment (8-15 hours per bid) deters them.

**How to use it:**
- Reference in the Executive Summary
- Feature in Work Product Samples section with caption explaining what it shows
- If buyer-specific data is involved, anonymize or use public datasets
- Match buyer's brand colors and visual identity
- Connect the sample to a specific RFP task or evaluation criterion

**Variant for sample-infeasible bids:** If timeline doesn't allow a sample build, lead instead with a screenshot library of past work. Don't just claim capability; show it.

### Tier 1.B — [Federal Intermediary] $[REDACTED]M federal program past performance

**Why it's a real discriminator (for federal-adjacent bids):** A multi-jurisdictional, federally-funded program lived in [Firm]'s analytics environment for two years and the reporting passed federal compliance review. Most competitive small firms can't credibly claim equivalent federal-grant-compliance experience.

**Why it passes the three tests for federal-adjacent bids:**
- Federal buyers care intensely about grant compliance experience because it's where projects fail audits.
- [Firm] can claim it (with citation).
- Many small firms don't have it.

**Important caveat:** For pure commercial or non-federal bids (some higher-ed, some private sector), this is a credential, not a discriminator. Don't lead with it on bids where federal compliance isn't relevant.

**How to use it:**
- Past performance section: lead CCAR
- Methodology section for any task involving multi-jurisdictional reporting, compliance reporting, or federal funds tracking
- Reference in Executive Summary when bid is federal-adjacent

## Tier 2 Conditional Discriminators (activate when context matches)

### Tier 2.A — [metro-local] presence

**Activates for:** [Regional Council], [Metro] regional councils, [Metro Area] municipalities, Missouri state contracts where local presence matters.

**Why it's conditional:** Regional councils exist to serve their member jurisdictions. A [Metro] firm bidding on [Regional Council] bids *to its own region*. A DC firm bidding on [Regional Council] is an outsider. This is real for regional/local bids.

**Meaningless for:** Federal civilian bids, out-of-state higher-ed, anywhere the buyer doesn't care about local presence.

**How to use it (when activated):**
- Firm Overview section: name [City]/[Metro] presence
- Methodology: reference availability for in-person meetings, local data familiarity
- Don't oversell — buyers expect it from local bidders, but it's an entry ticket more than a winning theme

### Tier 2.B — 8(a) certification (future-state)

**Activates when:** SBA 8(a) certification is granted (currently in progress with two-year waiver via [APEX Counselor] at [APEX Office]).

**Why it will be a real discriminator:** 8(a) firms can win sole-source contracts up to $4.5M that competitors cannot bid on. That's not "we're better than them" — that's "they can't legally compete here." It's the strongest type of discriminator.

**How to use it (once activated):**
- Reps & Certs / firm overview: prominently disclose 8(a) status
- Target sole-source pathways via agency Office of Small and Disadvantaged Business Utilization (OSDBU)
- Look at agency 8(a) spending goals — federal agencies have 5% targets

## Strengths (use as proof points, NOT as headline win themes)

These are strong features that support discriminators but don't carry a bid on their own. Use them in supporting paragraphs, technical skills sections, and methodology — not as the main pitch.

- **Microsoft-native stack.** Power BI, Azure, SharePoint, Fabric. Most analytics firms are Tableau-default or generalist. [Firm] is Microsoft-first. When the buyer uses Microsoft (most do), this is a fit signal worth mirroring.
- **Direct principal access.** [Principal] does the work, not a junior. Useful as a proof point on responsiveness. Don't lead with it — every small firm says this and buyers have heard it.
- **Named GIS subcontractor ([GIS Subcontractor], [GIS-certified]).** Beats "we will identify a GIS specialist." Useful for any bid with GIS scope. But it's a feature — any firm can sub a GIS person.
- **Two-week turnaround capability.** Functional reality, not a strategic advantage. Buyers care about quality and reliability more than turnaround speed.
- **Federal grant compliance fluency.** A skill, not a discriminator on its own. Becomes Tier 1.B when paired with [Federal Intermediary] past performance and the bid is federal-adjacent.

## Win Theme Construction (per Shipley)

A win theme is **not** a list of [Firm] strengths. A win theme is a sentence that says:

> "Section M evaluation factor [X] is best served by [Firm]'s [discriminator/strength] because [proof point]."

### Template

```
Your [evaluation criterion from Section M] requires [what the buyer needs].
[Firm] meets this with [specific capability/discriminator],
demonstrated by [specific past performance or proof],
and concretely shown in [working sample, named subcontractor, etc.].
```

### Example for [Regional Council]

> "[Regional Council]'s RFP evaluates 'demonstrated technical skills in data analytics, planning, and GIS.' [Firm] meets this with proven Microsoft-native analytics capability ([Federal Intermediary] $[REDACTED]M federal program, $[REDACTED]M enterprise sales pipeline) and a named [GIS-certified] GIS subcontractor ([GIS Subcontractor]), and is demonstrating it concretely via the regional situational awareness dashboard prototype included as Appendix C."

Notice: this theme is anchored to a specific evaluation criterion ("demonstrated technical skills in data analytics, planning, and GIS"), names the discriminators that apply, and provides concrete proof.

### Example for UM

> "[State University]'s RFP requires 'demonstrated understanding of the University System's measurement framework.' [Firm] meets this with explicit study of [State University]'s buyer's existing measurement framework, and demonstrates this understanding through the conceptual [Named Dashboard Product] mockup included as Appendix B — built on the Power BI / Microsoft Fabric architecture aligned with [Internal Analytics Office]'s stated direction."

## How to Pick Win Themes for a Bid

During discovery, extract Section M evaluation criteria (or the equivalent). For each criterion, ask:

1. Which Tier 1 or Tier 2 discriminator applies?
2. Which [Firm] strength supports that discriminator?
3. What concrete proof can I attach (past performance, sample, named partner)?
4. Can I write a one-sentence theme that connects all three?

Aim for 3-5 win themes per bid, one per major evaluation criterion. Each theme should be repeatable across multiple sections (Executive Summary, Methodology intro, Past Performance) so evaluators hear it more than once.

## Ghost-the-competition language

When there's a known or likely incumbent, write to the evaluator's frustrations with them. Don't name competitors. Examples of indirect language that positions:

- "Effective regional analytics requires more than a generalist consultancy bolting BI onto a transportation toolkit. [Firm] is BI-first, with planning context, not the reverse."
- "Smaller firms sometimes promise principal-led delivery and then hand off to juniors after contract signing. The resume in this proposal is who shows up to your meetings."

Use this carefully — overdone, it sounds petty. Once per proposal, in the Executive Summary or Approach intro, is plenty.
