# Agency Pattern — SBA 8(a) Sole-Source Pathway

For pursuing federal contracts via SBA 8(a) Business Development Program, once [Firm]'s 8(a) certification is granted.

**Current status:** [Firm]'s 8(a) application is in progress with a two-year waiver, supported by [APEX Counselor] at [APEX Office]. The two-year operational history requirement is being waived because [Firm] was founded June 2025. Until certification lands, this entire pathway is *future-state*.

## What 8(a) certification unlocks

**Sole-source contracts up to $4.5M** (general services NAICS) or **$7M** (manufacturing NAICS). Federal agencies can award these without competitive bidding — meaning [Firm] isn't competing against other firms once an agency wants to work with [Firm].

**Set-aside competitions** restricted to 8(a) firms. Smaller competitive pool.

**Mentor-protégé eligibility** — formal partnership with established federal primes who provide development support in exchange for joint-venture access. Most 8(a) firms pursue this within their first 2-3 years.

**Agency 8(a) goals.** Federal agencies have small business and 8(a) spending goals (5% small-disadvantaged-business is the federal-wide goal). Agencies actively seek 8(a) firms to meet these targets — especially in late fiscal year (Q4: July-September) when goal pressure peaks.

## Why this is the strongest discriminator type

Unlike "we're better at X than competitors," 8(a) sole-source means competitors can't legally bid. It's not "we win the comparison" — it's "there is no comparison." That's the strongest discriminator in government contracting.

## Caveat: 8(a) doesn't mean automatic awards

Common misconception. 8(a) status alone doesn't guarantee work. What it does:

1. Makes [Firm] *eligible* for sole-source awards an agency wants to make
2. Restricts who can compete in 8(a) set-aside competitions
3. Helps agencies meet small-disadvantaged-business spending goals

What it doesn't:

1. Generate inbound contracts automatically
2. Replace the need for relationships with agency program offices
3. Eliminate proposal-writing or pricing competition
4. Cover work outside [Firm]'s NAICS codes

The work of 8(a) is still relationship building, capability statement marketing, and being visible to agency OSDBU offices and program managers.

## Path to first sole-source

Once certified, the playbook:

### Step 1 — OSDBU outreach

Every federal agency has an Office of Small and Disadvantaged Business Utilization (OSDBU). They maintain databases of certified firms and brief program offices on 8(a) firms available.

**Targets aligned with [Firm]'s capabilities:**

- HHS OSDBU — federal grant analytics is a strength fit
- GSA OSDBU — broad civilian agency reach
- EPA OSDBU — civilian, smaller-scale
- DOT OSDBU — transportation analytics
- USDA OSDBU — extension and program analytics
- Education (ED) OSDBU — federal program reporting

**Outreach approach:**
1. Capability statement (one-page, [Firm]'s NAICS, past performance summary, key differentiators)
2. Request for introduction to relevant program offices
3. Follow up with 1-1 meeting (often virtual, 30 minutes)
4. Capability statement gets distributed by OSDBU to interested program offices

### Step 2 — Program office relationship

Once OSDBU connects [Firm] to a program office, the relationship work begins:

- Initial briefing on [Firm]'s capabilities and past performance
- Discussion of agency pain points and upcoming procurement plans
- Capability demonstration (this is where [Firm]'s working-sample discriminator works)
- Sources-sought / RFI responses to signal interest in specific upcoming work
- Eventually, a sole-source justification by the program office to the contracting officer

### Step 3 — Sole-source justification

For sole-source 8(a) awards under $4.5M general services, the agency CO can award without competition if the requirement matches [Firm]'s NAICS and the program office can document why [Firm] is the right firm.

The proposal then is more like a scope-and-cost document than a competitive proposal — the technical and management approach still need to be solid, but win themes against competitors don't apply (there aren't any).

## Mentor-Protégé Program

Worth considering 6-12 months into 8(a) status:

- Formal SBA-approved partnership with an established federal prime
- Prime provides business development support, training, technical capability access
- In exchange, prime can joint-venture with [Firm] on 8(a) set-asides (without losing [Firm]'s 8(a) sole-source eligibility)
- Joint ventures can pursue work above [Firm]'s standalone capacity

Candidate mentor types:
- Mid-tier federal primes in [Firm]'s NAICS who don't have 8(a) status
- Large primes seeking diversification of their small-biz utilization
- Established 8(a) graduates (firms that aged out of 8(a)) who can mentor

## 8(a) bid pattern (when 8(a) status is referenced in proposal)

When responding to an 8(a) set-aside or sole-source:

**Cover page:** Prominently disclose 8(a) certification number and dates.

**Firm Overview:** First sentence references 8(a) certification. NAICS codes. SAM.gov registration.

**Reps & Certs:** All small-business and 8(a) certifications completed.

**Past Performance:** Frame [Firm]'s experience around the 8(a) mission alignment — small-disadvantaged-business development, capability scaling.

**Pricing:** Still competitive (8(a) doesn't mean overcharging). Sole-source awards face fair-and-reasonable pricing review.

## Anti-patterns specific to 8(a)

- **Don't assume 8(a) certification eliminates the need for capability building.** It's a market-access tool, not a substitute for execution.
- **Don't overcommit on capacity.** 8(a) firms that take on too much and fail to deliver lose the program faster than firms that grow modestly.
- **Don't ignore the 9-year clock.** 8(a) certification lasts 9 years total (4 years developmental + 5 years transitional). Plan toward graduation by building non-8(a) past performance and revenue.
- **Don't lose track of compliance.** 8(a) firms must meet ongoing reporting (annual reviews, business activity reports). Missing these triggers termination.
- **Don't pursue work outside NAICS codes.** 8(a) eligibility is per NAICS. Pursuing work in unauthorized NAICS undermines the certification.

## Outlook on 8(a) approval timeline

SBA 8(a) application processing typically takes 90-120 days from complete submission. Common rejection reasons:

- Incomplete documentation
- Insufficient evidence of social/economic disadvantage
- Insufficient evidence of operational viability (this is what the waiver addresses)
- NAICS or size standard issues

Once approved:
- Letter of acceptance to SBA 8(a) program
- 4-year developmental phase begins
- Annual reviews required
- Mentor-protégé available once relationships develop
