# Voice and Tone

These rules are non-negotiable for any [Firm] proposal content. They were locked in during the [Regional Council] bid and apply across every future bid.

## Five Core Rules

1. **Use active voice and a confident, conversational, professional tone.**
2. **Never use the banned phrases.**
3. **Vary sentence structure** — combine short, punchy sentences with longer, detailed ones.
4. **Use contractions** (we'll, it's, we're, won't, can't).
5. **Avoid the "X isn't just about Y" / "X is not just Y" construction.**

## Banned Phrases (zero tolerance)

These phrases are AI-tells and signal generic consulting copy. Never use them in any [Firm] content:

- **delve / delve into**
- **leverage / leveraging**
- **unlock / unlocking**
- **comprehensive**
- **game-changer / game-changing**
- **"in today's digital age"** or any "in today's [adjective] world" opener
- **"X isn't just about Y"** / **"X is not just Y"** constructions
- **"navigate the complexities of..."**
- **"streamline / streamlined"** (overused)
- **"robust"** (overused, often meaningless)
- **"empower / empowering"**
- **"in the realm of..."**
- **"it goes without saying..."**
- **"foster / fostering"** (when used corporate-style, like "foster collaboration")
- **"holistic"** (almost always a red flag)
- **"synergy / synergies"**
- **"cutting-edge"**
- **"state-of-the-art"**
- **"world-class"**
- **"best-in-class"**
- **"value proposition"** (in proposal body — fine in cost sections)
- **"deliver value"** / **"value-added"**
- **"ensure"** (overused; replace with concrete commitment)
- **"strive to"** / **"endeavor to"**
- **"facilitate"** when "help" or "run" would work
- **"utilize"** when "use" would work

## Allowed but Use Carefully

Some words become AI-tells when stacked. Limit per page:

- "approach" — fine once or twice; replace with "method," "way," "plan" otherwise
- "solution" — fine for technical proposals; flag if appearing in every paragraph
- "capability" / "capabilities" — fine for a capability statement; limit elsewhere
- "stakeholders" — fine but specific names better when possible

## Tone Calibration

The [Firm] voice is:

- **Direct.** "We won't disappear for three weeks and reappear with a draft." Not: "We will ensure consistent communication throughout the engagement."
- **Confident without consulting-speak.** "We've read the RFP carefully and we're not proposing a generic analytics engagement." Not: "Our team brings extensive expertise."
- **Slightly blue-collar.** Plain words over fancy. "We'll embed with [Regional Council] Emergency Services staff from day one." Not: "Our team will integrate seamlessly with stakeholders."
- **Specific.** Names, numbers, dates, tools. "[GIS Subcontractor] holds a current ESRI certification (issued [Year]) and has completed [N] government GIS projects with a [X]% success rate."
- **Plainly competent.** No hedging. "Both are covered." not "We believe we have strong capabilities in both areas."

## Examples — Real [Firm] voice

These are real sentences from the [Regional Council] proposal that hit the voice right:

> "We've read the RFP carefully and we're not proposing a generic analytics engagement. What [Regional Council] needs is a team that can simultaneously support planning documentation, build operational dashboards, integrate data across systems, and hand everything off clean by September 18. Here's how we'll do it."

> "We'll embed with [Regional Council] Emergency Services staff from day one. That means attending planning sessions, capturing operational data, and turning it into usable documentation fast. We won't disappear for three weeks and reappear with a draft."

> "[Regional Council]'s RFP specifically references Microsoft-based tools and the ESRI platform. Both are covered."

Notice: contractions, short declarative sentences, concrete actions, specific dates, no jargon.

## Sentence Structure Rules

- **Vary length aggressively.** A page full of medium-length sentences reads AI-generated. Mix short (5-10 words) with longer (15-25 words). Occasional very short sentences ("Both are covered.") punch.
- **Lead with subject and action.** Not "It is our intention to provide..." but "We'll provide..."
- **Cut throat-clearing openings.** Not "It should be noted that..." just say the thing.
- **Use first person plural.** "We" not "[Firm]" in most paragraph copy. "[Firm]" is fine when introducing the firm; otherwise we/our.
- **End with the point.** Don't trail off with explanatory clauses that dilute the closing impression.

## What to Watch For in Edits

When reviewing AI-drafted or fast-drafted prose for voice compliance:

1. Search for each banned phrase. Replace.
2. Search for "X is not just Y" / "X isn't just about Y" patterns. Rewrite.
3. Read aloud — does it sound like a human consultant talking, or like a corporate-speak generator?
4. Count sentence lengths in a paragraph. Are they all 12-18 words? Vary them.
5. Check for contractions. If none, add them.
6. Check for passive voice clusters. Most should be active.

## Note on Cover Letters and Executive Summaries

These are the most often AI-detected sections. Spend extra time here. The opening sentence sets the tone for the entire read.

Strong opener pattern (from [Regional Council]):
> "We've read the RFP carefully and we're not proposing a generic analytics engagement."

This works because: contraction, specific reference to RFP, sets up by saying what we're NOT doing first, conversational tone. It does not sound like an AI wrote it.

Weak opener patterns (avoid):
> "[Firm Name] is pleased to submit this proposal..."
> "In response to your RFP, we are excited to present our comprehensive solution..."
> "Our team brings deep expertise across data analytics, business intelligence, and visualization..."

These all sound generated. Don't write them.
